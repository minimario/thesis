In \dataset, we study the ability of language models to solve olympiad-style inequality
problems. While previous evaluations in mathematics such as GSM8K \cite{cobbe2021training} and 
MATH-500 \cite{hendrycks2021measuring} focus on final-answer correctness, we design \dataset
to measure step-wise reasoning correctness in addition to final-answer accuracy. By doing so,
we discover what we call a \emph{soundness gap}: models often arrive at the correct final answer
while hallucinating bogus reasoning steps, reinforcing the importance of evaluating based on the
correct metric.

\subsection{Task Setup, Dataset, and Metrics}
An inequality problem generally involves proving that a specified inequality
holds under given conditions, such as \textit{prove $a + b \geq 2\sqrt{ab}$ for all
positive real numbers $a$ and $b$}. In \dataset, we reformulate inequality proof problems into two verifiable
subtasks: \textbf{bound estimation} and \textbf{relation prediction}. The bound
estimation task asks for an optimal constant in a given inequality. For example,
in $a + b \geq C\sqrt{ab}$ for all $a,b>0$, the objective is to find the largest
valid value of $C$. An example of such a task is shown below.
The relation prediction task asks the model to determine
the correct relationship between two expressions. For instance, given
$f(\mathbf{x})=a+b$ and $g(\mathbf{x})=2\sqrt{ab}$, the goal is to identify the
relation, in this case $\ge$, that holds for all $a,b>0$.

\input{paper-includes/ineqmath/src/figure_training_data_example_bound}

We split inequality proofs into these two subtasks because
they simplify the evaluation process while retaining the core challenges inherent 
in the original problems. In total, \dataset contains a 1,252-problem training corpus 
split evenly between bound-estimation and relation-prediction tasks, as well as a
test set contains 200 olympiad-level benchmark problems.

To measure the accuracy of a problem, we use a final-answer judge in conjunction
with a suite of step-wise proof soundness judges.
The \textbf{final-answer judge} is similar to that used in previous math benchmarks:
it extracts the final answer and checks mathematical equivalence to the
reference answer, as done in MATH-500 \citep{hendrycks2021-apps}.
The step-wise proof soundness judge checks for four common forms of unsound
reasoning. The \textbf{toy-case judge} flags solutions that infer a universal
claim from a few numerical examples. The \textbf{logical-gap judge} flags
missing derivation steps, unjustified transformations, and unsupported
optimality claims. The \textbf{numerical-approximation judge} flags cases where
decimal approximations replace exact symbolic reasoning in a way that affects
the proof. The \textbf{numerical-computation judge} checks arithmetic and
substitution errors once concrete quantities appear.

We report three different metrics. \emph{Answer accuracy} only requires the final
answer to be correct. \emph{Step accuracy} requires the reasoning trace
to pass all four step-wise checks. \emph{Overall accuracy}
requires both, and is the strongest check ensuring that both the answer and the
proof reasoning steps are correct.

In Table~\ref{tab:main_results}, we show the performance of 24 models on \dataset,
revealing several insights into current LLM capabilities for inequality proving:


\textbf{1) Reasoning LLMs achieve higher final-answer accuracy.} First, we compare
reasoning models with their general-purpose chat counterparts. We see that o1 (62.5\% \textit{Answer Acc}) and 
Grok 3 mini (71.5\% \textit{Answer Acc}) significantly outperform GPT-4o (37.5\%) and Grok 3 (54.5\%) in 
final-answer accuracy.


\textbf{2) Step-wise scrutiny reveals a dramatic performance drop.} 
Second, we discover a large soundness gap across all models. As we show in Figure~\ref{fig:ineqmath-key-results}, 
the \textit{Overall Acc} plummets when steps are evaluated. For instance, Grok 3 mini's accuracy 
drops from 71.5\% \textit{Answer Acc} to 6.0\% \textit{Overall Acc}.

\textbf{3) Robust proof construction remains a major challenge.} Third, we reveal that
providing a mathematically rigorous proof remains a significant challenge for all models.
Even the best model, Gemini 2.5 Pro, only achieves 43.5\% \textit{Overall Acc}, highlighting
that solving challenging inequality proofs remains a challenge for these LLMs.

\textbf{4) Scaling does not reliably close the soundness gap.} Finally, we investigate 
whether scaling up model size or test-time computation can help close the soundness gap. 
The answer is largely no, as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:ineqmath-scaling}. Larger models do tend 
to improve final-answer accuracy but do not help with overall accuracy. 
Similarly, increasing the maximum token budget of models can initially help, but quickly plateau,
as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ineqmath-test-time-scaling}.

\subsection{Discussion}
Because it is much more difficult to evaluate the soundness of an entire mathematical proof compared
to checking a final answer, many mathematics benchmarks have focused solely on the latter. However,
through \dataset, we saw that evaluating both accuracies reveals a blind spot of previous evaluations,
as models showed up to a 65.5\% drop between final-answer accuracy and overall accuracy.
More broadly, our results show that benchmarks are only as informative as the metric 
that it measures. While final-answer accuracy is easy to measure, it does not capture the capability 
that we ultimately care about: generating sound mathematical proofs.
