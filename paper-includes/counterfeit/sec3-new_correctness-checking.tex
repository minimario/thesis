\subsection{Can code language models understand counterfeit samples?} \label{sec:understanding-counterfeit}
In this section, we showed that models struggled to understand counterfeit samples. The figures highlight representative datasets and models; the qualitative conclusions were robust across the evaluated benchmark/model combinations.
Unlike prior correctness-checking, execution-prediction, and self-repair evaluations, our evaluation tests a more challenging scenario. Because the negative examples are counterfeit rather than trivially broken, success requires recognizing subtle 
semantic failures rather than spotting simple syntax errors or obvious test failures.

\subsubsection{Correctness Checking}
We began by examining whether language models could correctly identify whether a program was correct or counterfeit given the natural language specification. In Fig. \ref{fig:execution-accuracy}, we plotted the accuracy of CodeLlama 34B, DeepSeek-Coder 33B, GPT-3.5, and GPT-4 on balanced datasets of correct and counterfeit programs for HumanEval and ODEX. For the first three models, the blue bars indicate that correctness checking accuracy was at about 60\% for both of these datasets, which was only slightly better than the 50\% random-guessing baseline. This indicated that models generally failed to distinguish between correct and counterfeit samples. In addition, by comparing the green and red bars, we observed that the performance of these three models on correct samples was much higher than their performance on counterfeit samples, showing that models were biased towards thinking that counterfeit samples were actually correct. On the other hand, GPT-4 was much better (but not perfect) at this task with an accuracy at around 80\% for both datasets. We also observed that, unlike the rest of the models, GPT-4 was \textit{not} biased towards predicting that these samples were correct. However, GPT-4 still faltered around 20\% of the time, and we qualitatively analyzed some of these remaining GPT-4 failures in Sec. \ref{sec:qual-analysis}.

\subsubsection{Execution Prediction}
Next, we assessed the ability of models to predict the execution behavior of counterfeit samples. In Fig. \ref{fig:execution-prediction-accuracy}, we plotted the execution accuracy of the previous four models on two datasets, LeetCode generated by DS-33B and HumanEval generated by CL-34B.

In this task, each sample included a program (correct or counterfeit) $P$ and an input $I$. The accuracy of the correct samples is shown in the green bars. Because counterfeit programs still passed a subset of tests, we distinguished their execution samples into two groups. We called samples where $P$ passed test $I$ \textit{test-passing} counterfeit samples and the rest \textit{test-failing} counterfeit samples. The execution prediction accuracies of these samples are shown in blue and red, respectively. The purple bars show the proportion of test-failing counterfeit samples where the model actually predicted the output of the correct program. Note that samples counting towards the red accuracy are disjoint from those counting towards the purple accuracy.

Overall, we observed that models had a difficult time distinguishing the semantics of a counterfeit program from their correct counterparts, suggesting they may have had a shallow understanding of program semantics. By comparing the green and blue bars with the red bar, we saw that models failed much more at executing counterfeit programs when the semantics were incorrect. The purple bars provide further evidence of this: models other than GPT-4 frequently executed counterfeit programs as if they had the semantics of a correct program, sometimes even more often than their true semantics (red). For GPT-4, the effect was much less pronounced but still present, as GPT-4 still performed much better on correct and test-passing counterfeits than test-failing counterfeits. Despite having such high performance, it was still confused for a sizable number of test-failing counterfeit samples and predicted the output of the correct program rather than the correct execution result. Overall, because models only saw the programs and not the problem statements, this suggested that they might have been hallucinating the semantics of incorrect programs. This provided further evidence that models were poor at distinguishing correct programs from counterfeit programs.

\subsubsection{Repair}
\label{sec:repair}
Finally, we probed the model's ability to repair the counterfeit samples it had generated.
Although this task may appear to simply boil down to code generation,
prior work has highlighted that code understanding forms an integral part of the repair pipeline since achieving good performance hinges on the
model's (in)ability to generate accurate textual explanations of \emph{what} is wrong with the code \cite{olausson2024repair}; as such, self-repair may give us further insight into the model's capabilities.

Prior work has shown that when given information about \emph{which} unit test failed, many models are capable of repairing incorrect Python programs at rates that exceed their baseline pass rates \cite{chen2024teaching,olausson2024repair}.
In this section, we pressed the model even harder by not giving any execution signal whatsoever, instead simply informing it that the program did not pass; thus, successful repair depended entirely on the model's own ability to understand the program and its relationship to the specification.
Importantly, the success rate of repair must be compared to the baseline pass@1 rate,
since a sample can also be ``repaired'' simply by drawing another unconditional sample from the model.

Fig.~\ref{fig:repair-accuracy} shows the results for CodeLlama 34B (specifically the instruction-tuned CodeLlama 34B-Instruct, since repair depends heavily on following instructions rather than simply regenerating code from scratch) and DeepSeek 33B when repairing their own programs on HumanEval and LeetCode (respectively).
In these figures, each point is the mean success rate of repair for a particular problem; points above the line $y=x$ (which corresponds to a pass rate equal to that of the simple resampling strategy) thus indicate successful repair, while points below it indicate that the model could not reliably debug and repair the programs.
We note that although repair appears somewhat successful with DeepSeek-33B on HumanEval (Fig.), beating out the baseline for 35/81 problems, in all other settings a strong majority of the points lie below the line $y=x$. In other words, the success rate of repair is---for most tasks---significantly below what one would achieve with the simple resampling strategy.
This evidence showed that models could not reliably repair counterfeit samples, which suggested that they could not understand why these programs were deemed incorrect.
