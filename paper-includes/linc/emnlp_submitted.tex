\begin{abstract}
% The ability to logically reason
Logical reasoning, i.e., deductively inferring the truth value of a conclusion given a set of premises, is an important task for artificial intelligence with wide potential impacts on science, mathematics, and society.
While many prompting-based strategies have been proposed to enable Large Language Models (LLMs) to do such reasoning more effectively,
they still appear unsatisfactory, often failing in subtle and unpredictable ways.
In this work, we investigate the validity of instead reformulating such tasks as modular neurosymbolic programming, which we call \texttt{LINC}: Logical Inference via Neurosymbolic Computation.
In \texttt{LINC}, an LLM acts as a semantic parser, translating premises and conclusions from natural language to expressions in first-order logic.
These expressions are then offloaded to an external theorem prover, which symbolically performs deductive inference.
Augmenting the open-source StarCoder+ model and GPT-3.5 in this way, we observe significant performance gains on tasks taken from the FOLIO and ProofWriter datasets.
With \texttt{LINC}, the comparatively small (15.5B parameters) StarCoder+ outperforms all our GPT-3.5 baselines, including \texttt{Chain-of-Thought} (\texttt{CoT}) prompting, by over 38\% on a balanced subset of ProofWriter.
Furthermore, augmenting GPT-3.5 with \texttt{LINC} achieves 97\% accuracy on this dataset, despite the fact that we evaluate in a domain transfer setting, in which the model is only given examples from FOLIO and then has to generalize to tasks in our subset of ProofWriter.

\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 \cite{brown2020language}, GPT-4 \cite{openai2023gpt4}, and PaLM \cite{chowdhery2022palm} have led to a series of remarkable successes in tasks ranging from text summarization to program synthesis. 
Some of these successes have encouraged the notion that such models are able to flexibly and systematically reason \cite{huang2022towards}.
This ability appears particularly salient when models are invoked using prompting strategies that explicitly encourage verbalizing intermediate reasoning steps before generating the final answer \cite{nye2021show,wei2022chain,kojima2022large,wang2022self}.
Yet, when inspected in detail, this ability to reason often appears to be  unreliable in practice.
For example, language models seem to struggle in synthetic reasoning tasks where they cannot rely on prior domain knowledge, e.g., by considering inferences over out-of-domain terms \citep{liang2022holistic}, and---perhaps more importantly---they do not seem to generalize well to long deductive chains, especially those composed with instances of negation \citep{anil2022exploring}. Furthermore, while the standard approach of ``scaling up'' seems to improve performance across some reasoning domains, other domains, e.g., reasoning involving use of Modus Tollens, show no such improvements \citep{mckenzie2022inverse}. 
These findings suggest that such models may be relying on approximate heuristics based on surface-level statistical patterns in reasoning tasks, rather than consistent, generalizable representations and strategies \citep{srivastava2022beyond, creswell2022selection}.

At the same time, the ability to accurately and soundly perform logical reasoning---which we define moving forward as performing valid logical deduction, moving from a set of premises through a chain of inferences to a set of valid conclusions---is important for AI and NLP due to its impact on downstream tasks. 
For example: retrieval-augmented chatbots may become more truthful if it can be verified that their answers logically follow from the retrieved facts;
data-driven models capable of logical reasoning may speed up progress across mathematics and the sciences through automated theorem proving and knowledge discovery;
and AI tutoring systems which ensure internal logical consistency might make for better educational platforms, teaching students to think more clearly and rigorously.
The question of how to enable state-of-the-art LLMs to become more reliable logical reasoners is thus one of great importance, with far-reaching implications.

Recently, language models have successfully been augmented with symbolic tools such as calculators, program interpreters, and search engines \citep{schick2023toolformer}, a symbiotic relationship which we conjecture will become increasingly favorable as models grow more effective at understanding how and when to use these symbolic tools. Inspired by this, we propose a new two-step neurosymbolic approach for end-to-end logical reasoning called \texttt{LINC}: \textbf{L}ogical \textbf{I}nference via \textbf{N}eurosymbolic \textbf{C}omputation. First, we use the language model to convert the natural language premises and conclusion into first-order logic (FOL) expressions \cite{barwise2000language, enderton2001mathematical}. Second, we use a FOL theorem prover to determine the validity of the conclusion. 
% \footnote{Concurrently, \cite{pan2023logic} proposed Logic-LM, a similar neurosymbolic approach. We highlight a few difference with their work in Sec. \ref{sec:related_work} and give a detailed comparison in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix-logiclm}.}

With our neurosymbolic approach, the language model no longer needs to perform deductive reasoning, as this is offloaded to the FOL theorem prover and can be executed with perfect accuracy. Instead, the difficulty with this approach is that the formalization from natural language to first-order logic needs to perfectly capture the information in the premises. As the relative difficulties of our formalization challenge compared to direct natural language reasoning is unclear, our core interest is to compare and contrast our neurosymbolic approach to logical reasoning with existing reasoning strategies like \texttt{Chain-of-Thought}, understanding the benefits and drawbacks to each approach. 


Toward this goal, our contributions are three-fold. First, we propose LINC, a two-stage neurosymbolic approach to solve logical reasoning tasks (Sec.~\ref{sec:ns-model}). Second, we compare our neurosymbolic approach with three baseline LLM strategies across three models (StarCoder+, GPT-3.5, GPT-4) and two datasets (FOLIO and ProofWriter). We show that our approach significantly improves performance over the baselines in all but one experimental condition, GPT-4 on FOLIO (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_discussion}). Third, we provide a thorough error analysis of our neurosymbolic approach with a particular focus on differentiating its mistakes to those of \texttt{Chain-of-Thought} prompting, highlighting the potential for a synergy of the two methods (Sec.~\ref{sec:error_analysis}). Overall, we present strong evidence for the premise of neurosymbolic logical reasoning systems based on language models and theorem provers.

\section{\texttt{LINC}: Logical Inference via Neurosymbolic Computation}
\label{sec:ns-model}
Our neurosymbolic approach to end-to-end logical deduction consists of two stages. In the first stage, the LLM acts as a semantic parser, translating NL statements into FOL expressions in our supported logic language.
In the second stage, these expressions are parsed from the LLM generation, and passed to an external automated theorem prover; we use \texttt{Prover9}, a high-performance prover widely used in the logic community \citep{prover9-mace4}.
The external solver either returns a value from the set \texttt{\{True, \texttt{False}, Uncertain\}} or encounters an error due to invalid FOL syntax (e.g. if the model fails to balance parantheses in the formulae).
The strengths of such an approach can be understood as a reformulation of the problem space.
While end-to-end NL-based reasoning allows for operation over a highly flexible expression space, the LLM is tasked with performing explicit deductive inference to arrive at a conclusion.
Using \texttt{LINC}, we trade off the flexible expression space of NL for syntactically strict logic programs, but in doing so, can leverage provable guarantees that deductive inference chains will be correct for the semantics of the generated logic expressions.
The challenge then becomes ensuring that 1) the logic expressions generated by the LLM are \textit{syntactically} valid, such that they are accepted by our prover, and 2) that they are \textit{semantically} valid, such that their evaluation results in the intended conclusion.
In order to mitigate failures due to the generation of invalid program expressions, we additionally introduce a $k$-way majority voting procedure (discussed further in \ref{sec:experiments}), whereby multiple candidate programs are sampled from the LLM in parallel, those with syntax errors are filtered out, and the most common conclusion among the remaining samples forms the candidate conclusion.
The significance of this problem space reformulation, beyond numerical increases in performance across a number of our experiments, can be appreciated by an in-depth evaluation of how \texttt{LINC} fails in comparison to traditional end-to-end LLM reasoning approaches, such as \texttt{Chain-of-Thought (CoT)}.
To foreshadow our results, we find that compared to \texttt{CoT}, \texttt{LINC} has worse recall but better precision on \texttt{True}/\texttt{False} predictions.
We discuss this further in Sec.~\ref{sec:error_analysis} and highlight \texttt{LINC}, as well as neurosymbolic computation more generally, as a potential approach to counter LLM overconfidence and \textit{hallucination}, resulting in assertions of incorrect conclusions.

\textbf{Datasets:} Our experiments use tasks from two existing datasets: \textbf{FOLIO} \cite{han2022folio} and \textbf{ProofWriter} \cite{tafjord2020proofwriter}, both of which have been shown to be challenging for off-the-shelf LLMs \cite{han2022folio, creswell2022selection}. FOLIO is an expert-written, open-domain, logically complex and diverse dataset for natural language reasoning with first-order logic. We use its validation set for our evaluation. However, of the 204 samples in the validation set, we discover that 22 have errors (details in Appendix \ref{appendix_folio_dataset}), leaving us with 182 examples for our evaluation. ProofWriter, meanwhile, is a synthetically generated dataset for logical reasoning over natural language. For our evaluation, we use the OWA (Open-World Assumption) portion of ProofWriter, since this setting best matches that of FOLIO. Since we are running a large number of experiments, we randomly select 360 data points to evaluate on in order to reduce costs. We sample these in such a way that the resulting data set is balanced across both the number of reasoning steps in the shortest ground truth proof (depth 0-5; 50 samples each) and across the three labels (\texttt{True}/\texttt{False}/\texttt{Uncertain}; 120 samples each; 20 each per depth).

\textbf{In-context learning examples:} We hand-pick eight diverse samples from the FOLIO training set to be used as few-shot in-context examples. Because ProofWriter does not come with ground truth FOL statements, we use these eight samples for both evaluations. Compared to questions in FOLIO, questions in ProofWriter generally have more premises per question (in our validation sets: an average of 5.3 in FOLIO vs. 18.8 in ProofWriter).
Thus, our evaluation on FOLIO is an \emph{in-distribution} task, whereas ProofWriter requires generalizing \emph{out-of-distribution} to reasoning over significantly larger sets of premises than are given in the prompt.

\textbf{Majority voting:} $K$-way majority voting, in which $K$ samples are taken i.i.d. from the model and the mode is used as the final prediction, has previously been shown to improve the performance of prompting-based strategies in logical reasoning tasks \cite{wang2022self}.
We implement such a strategy in our work, with reported accuracies reflecting $K=10$-way majority voting, unless otherwise stated.
In the case of ties between two labels, we arbitrarily select the first of the two to have been generated.
We report the effect of $K$ on performance across our conditions and briefly discuss trends in Appendix \ref{appendix:k_way}.

\textbf{Controlled Baselines:}
\label{sec:controlled-baselines}
We compare \texttt{LINC} to three baselines, which we call \texttt{Na\"ive}, \texttt{Scratchpad}, and \texttt{Chain-of-Thought (CoT)}.
In the \texttt{Na\"ive} baseline, the model is given the natural language premises and is asked to directly generate the label (\texttt{True}/\texttt{False}/\texttt{Uncertain}).
In the \texttt{Scratchpad} baseline \cite{nye2021show}, the model is asked to first generate FOL expressions corresponding to the premises, and then generate the label.
This baseline is thus an ablation of \texttt{LINC}, where we use the model instead of \texttt{Prover9} as the logic solver.
Finally, in the \texttt{Chain-of-Thought (CoT)} baseline, we use the standard technique of \texttt{CoT} prompting \cite{wei2022chain,kojima2022large,wang2022self}, where the model is asked to generate step-by-step natural language reasoning to arrive at the conclusion.
The prompts we use for all approaches can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_folio_prompts}.

We find that \texttt{Scratchpad}, where the intermediate logical formulae are still generated but the call to the symbolic solver is ablated and replaced by the model's own prediction, does not appear to benefit performance; for StarCoder+, neither \texttt{scratchpad} nor the \texttt{na\"ive} baseline perform better than simply deterministically predicting the most common label (``\texttt{Uncertain}'').
For GPT-3.5, the trend is similar, although the gap between \texttt{LINC} and the closest baseline shrinks (62.6\% vs 54.9\% average accuracies).
For GPT-4, the trend reverses: \texttt{LINC} underperforms \texttt{CoT}. However, we perform a McNemar's test to get the p-value on this GPT-4 \texttt{LINC} vs \texttt{CoT} comparison, and we find that the difference is not significant ($p=0.58$). Meanwhile, for our balanced subset of ProofWriter, we see significant performance gains across the board (Figure \ref{fig:results-pw}) and particularly so for GPT-3.5, which achieves an accuracy of 96.4\% when paired with LINC.\footnote{Given the strong performance of GPT-3.5 with LINC, we elect not to evaluate GPT-4 on the ProofWriter tasks (of which there are twice as many as we have tasks from FOLIO) in order to reduce costs.}

In light of the high accuracies obtained with \texttt{LINC} on ProofWriter, we offer two plausible reasons why \texttt{LINC} is particularly favorable on this dataset.
Firstly, ProofWriter is---unlike FOLIO---completely synthetically generated, with relatively short sentences, perhaps lending itself particularly well to being formalized in FOL.
However, it is noteworthy that the \texttt{scratchpad} mode does not seem to improve performance over the \texttt{na\"ive} baseline, indicating that even if the NL-to-FOL task were particularly easy in this domain, this is not something that the model is itself capable of leveraging to improve its predictions.
The second reason might be that in this \emph{out-of-distribution} setting, in which the model must generalize to a larger set of premises with potentially longer deductive chains than those found in the prompt, the baseline strategies struggle. This distribution shift makes it harder for the model to ignore irrelevant premises in the question and carry out all deductive chains correctly.
Nonetheless, in \texttt{LINC}, the symbolic solver robustly handles irrelevant premises and long deductive chains, since the LLM only needs to translate each sentence into FOL.

To test this last explanation further, we plot each model's performance across ProofWriter as a function of the necessary proof depth in Figure~\ref{fig:results-qdep}.
We note first that StarCoder+'s performance remains flat and close to chance with all three baseline methods (Figure~\ref{fig:starcoder-qdep}). Meanwhile, \texttt{LINC} performance remains far above chance, although it drops somewhat as necessary proof depth increases; this performance drop indicates that StarCoder+ struggles somewhat with the NL-to-FOL translation task as the problem at hand gets larger.
On the other hand, for GPT-3.5, all baselines perform above chance at proof depth 0 (i.e., where the conclusion can immediately be reached from the premises), but quickly drops back down (Figure~\ref{fig:gpt-3.5-qdep}). While \texttt{Chain-of-Thought} prompting allows the model to complete some depth-1 tasks, even this strategy then immediately drops down to chance (within 1 standard deviation).
When augmented with \texttt{LINC}, however, GPT-3.5 is able to achieve near-perfect performance across all proof depths, providing evidence for the scalability of this approach to longer deductive chains.

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:error_analysis}
% In the previous section, we saw that \texttt{LINC} outperforms all of the baselines by a significant margin on ProofWriter, and that part of the reason why is that it allows the model to scale to much longer deductive chains.
% However, this begs the question of why \texttt{LINC} was \emph{not} found to increase performance that much in FOLIO, particularly for GPT-4.
Next, we move to our core question: \textit{How does the behavior of our neurosymbolic reasoning approach differ from that of in-context reasoning approaches}?
We focus on comparing GPT-4 \texttt{CoT} vs. GPT-4 \texttt{LINC} on FOLIO, since these are the best performing settings (75.3\% vs. 72.5\% average accuracy). We leave an analysis on StarCoderPlus FOLIO to Appendix \ref{sec:starcoder_error_folio}.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis} \label{sec:error_qualitative}
Qualitatively, we find that \texttt{LINC} and \texttt{CoT} have completely different failure modes. We give a high-level overview and abbreviated examples of each failure mode here, leaving full detailed examples to Appendix \ref{appendix_folio_error}. First, we detail the failure modes for \texttt{LINC}:

\textbf{L1. FOL fails to capture implicit information not mentioned in the premises.} Often, there is obvious information not explicitly listed in the premises that is necessary to explicitly encode in FOL in order to successfully make a desired deduction. In the example below, we must encode in FOL the implicit knowledge that Harry is a person (\texttt{Person(Harry)}).

\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \small Premise 1: \texttt{When a person reads a book, that person gains knowledge.}
    \item[] \small FOL: \texttt{all x. all y. (Person(x) \& Reads(x, y) \& Book(y) -> Gains(x, Knowledge))}
    \item[] \small Premise 2: \texttt{Harry read the book "Walden" by Henry Thoreau.}
    \item[] \small FOL: \texttt{Reads(Harry, Walden)}
    \item[] \small Conclusion (Prover9: Uncertain): \texttt{Harry gains knowledge.}
    \item[] \small FOL: \texttt{Gains(Harry, Knowledge)}
\end{itemize}


\textbf{L2: FOL fails to capture information explicitly mentioned in the premises due to the choice of representation.} Even when information is explicitly written in the premises, the choice of how the NL is represented in FOL can lead to lost information. In the example below, the fact that Heinrich was a Nazi German politician is captured by one symbol \texttt{NaziGermanPolitician}, causing the information that he was independently Nazi, German, or a politician to be lost.
As a result, \texttt{LINC} predicted \texttt{Uncertain} instead of the ground truth label \texttt{True}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \small Premise: \texttt{Heinrich Scmidt was a Nazi German politician.}
    \item[] \small FOL: \texttt{NaziGermanPolitician (HeinrichSchmidt)}
    \item[] \small Conclusion (Prover9: Uncertain): \texttt{Heinrich Schmidt was German.}
    \item[] \small FOL: \texttt{German(HeinrichSchmidt)}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{L3: FOL contains syntax errors.}  Across all generations, we find that the FOL expressions sometimes contain syntax errors: 38\% for StarCoderPlus, 24\% for GPT-3.5, and 13\% for GPT-4. The most common error is that the same symbol is used with multiple arities. As an example, if \texttt{Summer(July4)} and \texttt{Loves(Alex, Summer)} were both present in a FOL translation, \texttt{Summer} would have a multiple arity violation.

Next, we detail three failure modes for \texttt{CoT}:

\textbf{C1. CoT concludes something different than it suggests.} One common mistake we often see with the CoT approach is that it generates a chain of reasoning indicating uncertainty with the conclusion, but then asserts that conclusion is false. An example of this is shown below, where GPT-4 suggests there is ``no evidence'' for the conclusion but then deduces it false.

\begin{itemize}
    % \item[] \small Premise 1: Some mammals have teeth.
    % \item[] \small Premise 2: Platypus have no teeth.
    % \item[] \small Premise 3: Platypus are mammals.
    % \item[] \small Premise 4: Humans have teeth.
    % \item[] \small Conclusion: Platypus are reptiles.
    \item[] \small \textbf{(GPT-4 Incorrect CoT):} Let's think step by step. We want to evaluate if platypus are reptiles. We know from the premises that platypus are mammals and that they have no teeth. The fact that some mammals have teeth does not contradict the fact that platypus, although mammals, do not have teeth. Moreover, the statement about humans having teeth is unrelated to the classification of platypus. There's no evidence in the premises that would suggest platypus are reptiles. Therefore, the statement is false.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{C2: CoT makes incorrect logical deductions.} While there are many instances where CoT makes incorrect logical deductions, one common deduction mistake is the structure ``if $B$ is true and $A \implies B$, then $A$ is true''. In the example shown below, we see this incorrect deduction where A is ``Mike has an F1 visa'' and B is ``Mike needs to apply for CPT or OPT if he wants to work in the US''. We note that making this type of logical mistake is often called the \emph{fallacy of the converse}, and that humans are not immune to it, either.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \small Premise 1: International students in US have either an F1 visa or a J1 visa.
    \item[] \small Premise 2: An international student in US with an F1 visa needs to apply for CPT or OPT if the student wants to work in the US.
    \item[] \small Premise 3: Mike is an international student.
    \item[] \small Premise 4: Mike needs to apply for CPT if he wants to work in the US.
    \item[] \small Conclusion (Ground Truth: Uncertain): Mike has an F1 visa.
    \item[] \small \textbf{(GPT-4 Incorrect CoT):} Let's think step by step. We want to evaluate if Mike has an F1 visa. From the premises, we know that Mike is an international student and needs to apply for CPT if he wants to work in the US. We also know that an international student in the US with an F1 visa needs to apply for CPT or OPT if the student wants to work in the US. So, it is clear that Mike has an F1 visa since he fits the criteria. Therefore, the conclusion is true.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{C3: CoT fails to find complex paths of reasoning.} We find that with all three models, CoT fails when the path of reasoning necessary to make the deduction is complex. Sometimes, CoT has difficulty getting started, and other times, it gets stuck in the middle of a reasoning chain.

\subsection{Quantitative Analysis}

\textbf{1. Compared to \texttt{CoT}, \texttt{LINC} has worse recall but better precision on True/False predictions.} To see this,
we plotted the confusion matrices in Figure~\ref{fig:gpt_cot_confusion} (\texttt{CoT}) and Figure~\ref{fig:gpt_neurosym_confusion} (\texttt{LINC}). 
Looking just at the distributions of predicted labels of the two methods, we see that \texttt{CoT} predicts 32\% \texttt{True}, 27\% \texttt{False}, 41\% \texttt{Uncertain}, while \texttt{LINC} predicts 24\% \texttt{True}, 17\% \texttt{False}, 57\% \texttt{Uncertain}, 2\% Error.
Notably, we observe that \texttt{LINC} predicts \texttt{Uncertain} much more frequently than \texttt{CoT} (57\% vs. 41\%).
To understand a potential reason why, recall that in L1 and L2, we saw that the translation from natural language to FOL loses information, meaning that the information conveyed through the FOL is often a subset of the information in the original premises. Removing these pieces of crucial information that were on the critical path to making True/False deductions may then leaves an uncertain conclusion. 
% \begin{lstlisting}[breaklines=true]
% Premise: Heinrich Scmidt was a Nazi German politician.
% Premise FOL, LINC GPT-4: \texttt{NaziGermanPolitician(HeinrichSchmidt)}
% Conclusion: Heinrich Schmidt was either German or Russian.
% Conclusion FOL, LINC GPT-4: German(HeinrichSchmidt) | Russian(HeinrichSchmidt)
% \end{lstlisting}
While the FOL translations sometimes don't retain all of the information in the NL, they rarely contain false information that was not provided in the original premises.
Therefore, \texttt{LINC}'s precision when predicting \texttt{True} or \texttt{False} is very high (93\%) compared to \texttt{CoT}'s 81\%, but the increase in unknown predictions comes at the cost of lower recall on True/False predictions (60\% \texttt{LINC} vs. 75\% \texttt{CoT}).

\textbf{2. \texttt{LINC} and \texttt{CoT} mispredict on different examples.} Earlier in Sec. \ref{sec:error_qualitative}, we saw that \texttt{LINC} and \texttt{CoT} exhibited different failure modes, which suggests they should fail on different examples. Indeed, this is the case.
Figure~\ref{fig:gpt_cot_neurosym} shows a $2\times2$ confusion matrix which compares whether or not each method's prediction was correct.
We observe that out of the $24+29+21=74$ samples where at least one method makes an incorrect prediction, only 21 are shared.
On a closer examination of these 21 samples, we find that 16 are ambiguous or incorrect in their specification (details in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_sharedmistakes}), so \textit{the two methods only disagree on 5 well-formed samples}.
This suggests that \texttt{LINC} and \texttt{CoT} are complementary methods which fail under distinct circumstances.

\textbf{3. Mispredictions of in-context reasoning baselines are more similar to each other than they are with mispredictions of \texttt{LINC}.} As an extension of the previous analysis, we next investigate the correlation between the mispredictions of each pair of methods.
% The finding that \texttt{LINC} and \texttt{CoT} appear to be failing under distinct circumstances invites the question of whether this is unique to \texttt{CoT}, or if it also holds true for the other baselines since they---like \texttt{CoT}---also perform implicit in-context reasoning.
To do so, we define a similarity score between two methods $A$ and $B$ as follows:
Given a dataset $D$ with $N$ rows and ground truth labels $\{A_i\}_{i=1}^N$ and $\{B_i\}_{i=1}^N$ from two methods $A$ and $B$, we define
$$\text{sim}_D(A, B) \triangleq \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}\left[ A_i = B_i \ne R_i \right] }{\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}\left[ A_i \ne R_i \text{ or } B_i \ne R_i\right]}$$
In words, $\text{sim}_D(A,B)$ measures the number of instances where $A$ and $B$ are wrong in identical ways vs. the number of instances where \emph{at least one} of them is wrong.

Figure~\ref{fig:method_similarity} shows the pairwise similarity between our four methods, highlighting that the similarity between \texttt{LINC}'s mispredictions and the other methods' mispredictions ($0.28$, $0.21$, $0.27$) is much lower than the similarity between any pair of in-context reasoning methods ($0.54$, $0.57$, $0.6$). 
% We see that \texttt{LINC} rarely makes the \emph{same} mispredictions as the baselines (column 1).
% We note that this is not simply because \texttt{LINC} makes fewer mistakes overall---which would bias our similarity score towards lower values---since we saw in Figure~\ref{fig:results-folio} that all methods are comparable on this dataset when used with GPT-4.
% On the other hand, when any of the baseline models predict the wrong answer, the other ones predict the \emph{same} wrong label in over 50\% of instances (middle two columns, below the diagonal).
These results suggest that for GPT-4 on FOLIO, the introduction of a symbolic solver with \texttt{LINC} is the only method we evaluate which significantly alters the ways in which the model \emph{fails} to reason.

\section{Related Work} \label{sec:related_work}

Our work contributes to the wider literature on eliciting natural language reasoning capabilities in models.
Although we have focused here on comparing a neurosymbolic approach to scratchpad \cite{nye2021show} and \texttt{Chain-of-Thought} prompting \cite{wei2022chain, kojima2022large, wang2022self}, many other similar or related techniques have been developed in recent years: least-to-most prompting \cite{zhou2022least}, selection-inference \cite{creswell2022selection}, backward chaining \cite{kazemi2022lambada}, and self-taught reasoning \cite{zelikman2022star}, to name a few. Some of these techniques have been formalized under the language model cascades framework \cite{dohan2022language}.
Our work is also closely related to prior work on using LLMs for programming \cite{chen2021evaluating}, particularly 
one line of work which shows that using a program as a symbolically grounded intermediate step can highly improve the performance of language models on tasks that require strong logical reasoning skills \citep{drori2022neural, gao2022pal}. 

When we introduce \texttt{LINC}, we claim to use an LLM as a semantic parser, and this notion is rested on a long tradition of semantic parsing research \cite{kamath2019survey}, whose aim is to map fragments of natural language into useful, symbolic meaning representations \cite{zelle1996learning, zettlemoyer2005learning, berant2013semantic, liang2013learning}. Unlike earlier works in this tradition, we use a language model to generate the semantic parse, which is a method under active investigations in recent years \cite{shin2021few, drozdov2022compositional, wang2023grammar}.

There has also been prior work on neurosymbolic-esque augmentations within the logical reasoning and factuality space.
\citet{zhang22improved} finetunes a language model to synthesize potential facts paired with likelihoods and then use a handwritten differentiable symbolic reasoner in order to deduce other facts. 
\citet{weir2022dynamic} also aim to augment language models with the ability to perform logical reasoning,
but instead of grounding the reasoning in a symbolic language train neural ``entailment'' models to decide if and how a given inference rule applies at each stage. 
% Alongside this prior work, we also recognize contemporary efforts in which language models have been augmented with symbolic reasoning systems.
Most similar to our set-up, \citet{pan2023logic}, which is contemporaneous to our work, pair \texttt{GPT-3.5} and \texttt{GPT-4} with external symbolic tools, including logic solvers, and evaluate on both FOLIO and ProofWriter (as well as two additional datasets).
Unlike our work, they also employ a self-refinement strategy to further boost their performance,
an approach that has recently shown promise across code generation and other NLP tasks
\cite{zhang2023selfedit,chen2023teaching,peng2023check,madaan2023selfrefine,olausson2023demystifying}.
Ultimately, we believe our contributions to be complementary to those of \citet{pan2023logic}:
while they show that carefully augmenting a state-of-the-art LLM with a symbolic logic solver has the potential to improve pass rates on a broad range of domains,
we focus here on quantitatively and qualitatively assessing how and when different large language models \emph{fail} to reason with and without access to an external solver.
We hope that this will provide a more detailed view of the relative strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

\section{Conclusion}

In this work, we present \texttt{LINC}: Logical Inference via Neurosymbolic Computation, a neurosymbolic approach for scalable logical reasoning with large language models. 
Our experiments show that LINC not only leads to significant performance gains in all but one setting, but also that it helps the model to generalize to questions which contain many more premises than the examples which it was shown in the prompt.
Finally, by carrying out a quantitative and qualitative analysis of the mistakes made by \texttt{LINC}, we find evidence that \texttt{LINC} may in fact be complementary to purely in-context reasoning strategies such as \texttt{Chain-of-Thoughts} prompting, as the types and frequencies of mistakes these strategies make differ greatly.
%, pointing to the potential for combining the two methods to achieve exceptional performance.

Then, we take a quantitative angle, showing that the mispredictions of the two approaches are highly different. We notice that the approaches often succeed when the other fails, suggesting the potential for combining the two methods to achieve exceptional performance.

\section{Limitations}

In this work, we have focused exclusively on logical \emph{deduction}, that is, predicting the truth value of a conclusion given a set of premises.
However, a broader notion of logical reasoning requires other elements, such as (1) inferring additional premises that would make an uncertain conclusion true and (2) producing proofs of the conclusion given the premises. 
% the inverse: logical \emph{induction}, i.e., moving from a given outcome to a possible set of explanations.
Also, while many fast algorithms (e.g. forward- and backward-chaining) exist for deduction, the general problem is still NP-hard. That means, even though our approach can in theory solves tasks with arbitrarily long deductive chains, very long ones may require unpractical amounts of time to solve. Moreover, first-order logic, the formalism used in this work, is not powerful enough to handle logical reasoning problems that require \textit{higher-order} logics. Alternative theorem provers are needed for those problems. Lastly, implementing our approach with both GPT models and the StarCoder+ model requires a non-trivial amount of resources. The former needs API access and can be expensive. For the latter, even though StartCoder+ is ``small'' compared to GPT models, it still contains billions of parameters and needs dedicated GPUs to run.
